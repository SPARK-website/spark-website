MENTAL AND EMOTIONAL SAFETY

Recognizing stress and anxiety:

  • Basically, stress is your body's response to challenging situations, and anxiety is constant worry or fear that affects your daily life.
  • Signs you might have stress or anxiety are trouble sleeping or concentrating, feeling restless and nervous, irritability, mood swings, and muscle tension.
  • You can cope with it by taking short breaks and moving your body, practicing deep breathing and meditation, or talking to a trusted individual.

Dealing with panic attacks:

  • A panic attack is a sudden feeling of intense discomfort or fear that can happen even without a real threat.
  • Signs someone could be having a panic attack are rapid heartbeat, short and rapid breaths, feeling out of control, and dizziness.
  • There are many methods to ground yourself during a panic attack. Some of these methods are:
    • 5-4-3-2-1 method: Notice 5 things you see, 4 you feel, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, and 1 that you taste.
    • Deep breathing: Inhale for 4 counts, hold your breath for 4, and then exhale for 4 counts. Repeat until you feel calm.
    • Use your senses: Hold an object and try to notice its weight, texture, and temperature.
  • Panic attacks are scary, but not dangerous. They will pass.

Coping with bullying:

  • Bullying can take many forms. It includes name-calling, teasing, spreading rumours, manipulation, exclusion and social isolation, or constant criticism.
  • You can deal with it by speaking up about it to a trusted adult or friend, documenting evidence like notes or messages, and most importantly, standing up for yourself.

Coping with toxic or abusive relationships:

  • Toxic and abusive relationships can take the form of constant criticism, manipulation, isolation from friends and family, physical threats or harm, controlling behaviour, and humiliation.
  • Tell a trusted adult, friend or counselor ASAP.
  • Limit interactions with the person as much as you can.
  • Identify safe spaces and people, and keep emergency numbers handy.
  • Don't feel embarrassed to seek professional help from therapists, helplines, or support groups.
