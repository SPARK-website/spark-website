About SPARK

SPARK stands for Safety, Protection, Awareness, Rescue, and Knowledge. This website is designed to help teens and young adults stay safe, learn basic self-defense, and handle emergencies confidently. This website is your go-to guide for self-defence and first aid tips. It provides simple step-by-step instructions on how to deal with injuries like cuts, fractures, burns, sprains, and much more. SPARK also teaches you some basic self-defence tips to keep you safe in public spaces and at home.

1. First Aid

Treating cuts, scrapes, and bruises

  • If you get a cut or a scrape, wash it immediately to prevent infection.
  • Stop the bleeding (if there is any) by pressing a clean cloth, tissue, or cotton swab firmly on the area.
  • Apply antiseptic or ointment (like Dettol, Savlon, or similar) if the wound is bigger or looks dirty.
  • Cover with a band-aid or clean dressing if it’s in an open area (like arms, legs, or hands) to keep germs out.
  • See a doctor if the wound is deep, won’t stop bleeding, or was caused by a rusty object.
  • If you are bruised, rest the area and don't bump it into anything.
  • Apply something cold to the area, such as an ice pack, for 10-15 minutes to reduce swelling.
  • Keep it raised if possible.
  • Avoid massaging the bruise.

Treating fractures, burns, and sprains

  • For fractures, do not move the injured part.
  • If possible, support the broken part with a splint wrapped in soft cloth.
  • Apply ice to reduce swelling.
  • Get medical help ASAP.
  • If sprained, then use R.I.C.E.: Rest the joint, Ice it for 10-15 minutes, Compress it with a soft cloth, but not too tightly, and **E**levate the joint (only if possible).
  • Call for medical help.
  • For burns, run it under cool water for 10-15 minutes.
  • Cover it using a clean cloth or gauze.
  • Don't pop blisters or apply ice, butter, or toothpaste.
  • Seek help if the burn is large, deep, or on sensitive areas like the face.

Helping someone who is choking or has fainted

  • If the person is choking and cannot speak or cough, act immediately.
  • Give 5 firm slaps between the shoulder blades.
  • Do the Heimlich Maneuver (stand behind them, with fist above belly button, and push inwards and outwards and repeat till object is out).
  • If they collapse or don't recover, call emergency.
  • If someone has fainted, lay them on their back.
  • Raise their legs slightly and loosen tight clothing.
  • If not awake in 2-3 minutes, call for medical help.
2. Self-Defence

Awareness and safety tips for walking alone at night or in public spaces

  • Stay alert and don't play loud music, which can distract you from your surroundings.
  • Stick to lit areas and don't go into dark alleys or corners.
  • Walk confidently; it will make you look less like a target.
  • Do NOT go too close to any vehicles; there is a possibility of being kidnapped.
  • Share your location with close friends or family when travelling alone at night.
  • Trust your gut and leave places you think are unsafe.

Using everyday objects to defend yourself

  • Hold your keys in between your fingers and slash or jab anyone who tries to attack.
  • Use perfume, pepper spray, sanitizer, etc., and spray it into the eyes of the assailant.
  • Use your bag or backpack to swing and create distance between you and the assailant.
  • Use water bottles or thermos bottles to swing at the attacker and aim for hands, arms, or shins.
  • Do not try to fight; run as fast as you can when you get a clear opportunity to.

If someone is following you

  • Stay calm and alert, and keep looking around.
  • Change direction and see if they are actually following you.
  • Go to busy places that are filled with people.
  • Call someone and let a friend or relative know your location.
  • Avoid shortcuts and stick to well-lit, crowded places.
  • Get to safety, but do not confront them.

How to escape holds and grabs

  • Make as much noise as possible to draw attention and make it clear that you are resisting the hold.
  • Aim for sensitive areas like eyes, throat, nose, knees, and groin.
  • Use your body weight and twist, drop, or push off to break their grip.
  • Use objects around you like keys, a water bottle, bags, etc.
  • As soon as you are free of their grip, run away as fast as you can, and do NOT look back.
  • Once you are in a safe location, call for help immediately (it can be friends, family, the police, or emergency services).

What to do in case of a kidnapping

  • Leave as much DNA as possible. If you are in a vehicle, touch as many places as you can to leave your fingerprints. Also, try getting the assailant's DNA on you; this will help when you report the incident later.
  • Observe your surroundings and look for any landmarks that can help you report the incident to authorities.
  • Stay calm and try humanizing yourself to the criminal.
  • Try to vomit or spit on the assailant; this might help loosen their grip on you.
  • Call or signal for help ASAP.
  • Try your best not to get into a vehicle with the kidnapper. Your chances of escape reduce when you are in a contained space where they can control you.
  • If in a vehicle, look for any escape opportunities (doors, windows, etc.).
  • Make noise to attract attention.
  • Thankfully, kidnapping is not as common now as it once was, but it is still a very serious issue, so make sure to stay prepared.

General safety tips

  • Always stay alert and scan your surroundings.
  • Keep essentials like your phone, keys, a whistle, and safety spray in easy reach.
  • Stick to well-lit and crowded routes, avoid shortcuts, and always inform someone where you are going.
  • Trust your instincts.
  • Speak up clearly if someone is invading your personal space or making you feel unsafe.
  • Do NOT accept food or drinks from strangers, or leave your food/drinks unattended in public places.

Personal safety apps

  • Noonlight offers emergency SOS, location tracking, and integration with devices like smoke alarms.
  • SoSecure by ADT provides emergency assistance, location sharing with guardians/emergency contacts, and an option to connect with a live agent.
  • The Sabre Personal Safety App allows you to order safety tools like pepper sprays and personal alarms.
3. Fire Safety

Basic tips on what to do in case of a fire

  • Stay calm and alert if there is a fire.
  • Always be aware of exits in your house, school, or public places.
  • If your clothes catch on fire, stop, drop to the ground, cover your face, and roll until the fire goes out.
  • Know the basics of using a fire extinguisher. If you don't, remember PASS (Pull pin, Aim at base, Squeeze, Sweep).
  • Dial your local emergency number immediately.
  • For extra safety, keep a small fire escape plan in your room so you always know where to go.

How to use a fire extinguisher

  • Just remember the acronym PASS.
  • Pull the pin to discharge the extinguisher.
  • Aim at the base of the fire instead of directly at the fire to extinguish it more effectively.
  • Squeeze the top handle or lever. This releases the extinguishing agent.
  • Sweep from side to side until the fire is out.
4. Cyber Security

What to post and not post online

Online safety might seem basic and obvious, but that doesn't make it any less of an important topic. It's best to avoid sharing any personal information about yourself, but the most important details, which you should never, under any circumstances, share to strangers on the internet (online friends don't count), are your address and phone number.


General tips to stay safe online

  • This is quite obvious, but still a very important rule. Do NOT click on suspicious links or download suspicious files. These attachments could contain viruses, worms, or other malware.
  • Only accept friend requests from people you know on social media. I'm sure everyone wants to have like a million followers on social media, but if you have an account on which you want to have thousands of followers, don't post personal things on it. Create a separate, private account that is only followed by people you know.
  • Last, but not least, if anyone, and I mean ANYONE (this includes friends and family), is harassing you or troubling you online, immediately block and report them. If you can, also report this to a trusted adult. Harassment is no joke, and is a very serious matter.
5. Mental and Emotional Safety

Recognizing stress and anxiety

  • Basically, stress is your body's response to challenging situations, and anxiety is constant worry or fear that affects your daily life.
  • Signs you might have stress or anxiety are trouble sleeping or concentrating, feeling restless and nervous, irritability, mood swings, and muscle tension.
  • You can cope with it by taking short breaks and moving your body, practicing deep breathing and meditation, or talking to a trusted individual.

Dealing with panic attacks

  • A panic attack is a sudden feeling of intense discomfort or fear that can happen even without a real threat.
  • Signs someone could be having a panic attack are rapid heartbeat, short and rapid breaths, feeling out of control, and dizziness.
  • There are many methods to ground yourself during a panic attack. Some of these methods are-
    • **5-4-3-2-1 method:** Notice 5 things you see, 4 you feel, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, and 1 that you taste.
    • **Deep breathing:** Inhale for 4 counts, hold your breath for 4, and then exhale for 4 counts. Repeat until you feel calm.
    • **Use your senses:** Hold an object and try to notice its weight, texture, and temperature.

  • Panic attacks are scary, but not dangerous. They will pass.

Coping with bullying

  • Bullying can take many forms. It includes name-calling, teasing, spreading rumours, manipulation, exclusion and social isolation, or constant criticism.
  • You can deal with it by speaking up about it to a trusted adult or friend, documenting evidence like notes or messages, and most importantly, standing up for yourself.

Coping with toxic or abusive relationships

  • Toxic and abusive relationships can take the form of constant criticism, manipulation, isolation from friends and family, physical threats or harm, controlling behaviour, and humiliation.
  • Tell a trusted adult, friend or counselor ASAP.
  • Limit interactions with the person as much as you can.
  • Identify safe spaces and people, and keep emergency numbers handy.
  • Don't feel embarrassed to seek professional help from therapists, helplines, or support groups.

How to find support if you feel unsafe or need help

  • Go to generally trustworthy adults like teachers or security staff.
  • If you feel unsafe, look for women with children, as they are more likely to help in these kinds of situations.
  • Always stay in populated areas.
  • Use your phone and call/text a trusted person immediately.
  • Always trust your instincts.
